Description

In recent years, Canada’s population growth has garnered significant media attention, particularly due to its rapid increase driven by immigration policies. Simultaneously, there has been global concern about declining birth rates in developed countries, especially in the West. This prompted me to delve into Canada's population statistics to explore current trends and uncover aspects of the country's demographic landscape that I hadn’t previously considered.

Examining Canada's population growth reveals a nearly consistent pattern of increase from 1946 up to 2023. It appears there is a slight deceleration in 2020, followed by an acceleration in 2021. Given that population growth is the sum of births and immigration minus deaths, and considering that 2020 marked the onset of the response to the COVID-19 pandemic, a plausible explanation for the deceleration could be a reduction in immigration and/or an increase in deaths. We'll investigate the number of births and deaths to determine if there was a significant deviation from the trend in 2020/2021 and observe the overall changes over time.

Looking at Canada's birth rates there appears to be a modest decline from 2019 to 2020, followed by a slight increase in 2021, although still within the broader cyclical patterns observed since the mid-20th century. These cycles are marked by a significant surge during the baby boomer era (1946-1964), a decline at the onset of Generation X (1965-1980), a resurgence with Millennials (1981-1996), and a dip during the early years of Generation Z (1997-2012), before stabilizing in the mid-2000s. The cyclical nature of these trends can be attributed to demographic shifts, where the large baby boomer population birthed the sizeable millennial generation, whereas the smaller generation X led to the relatively smaller generation Z. However, the pattern shows signs of weakening, likely influenced by immigration and the resultant births outside these historical cycles. Next lets look at mortality rates to see if there have been any significant patterns to investigate.

The death rate in Canada seems to have been steadily increasing at a consistent pace until around the early 2010s when the rate of death appears to be increasing from its long term trend. This relative increase may be the result of the relatively large baby boomer generation starting to die off. Let's also look at the combined effects of birth and death on the Canadian population so that we can look at natural change in population without immigration.

Without immigration, Canada appears to be on track for a declining population within the next 5 to 10 years. This trend has already been observed in some Asian and European countries and is expected to become a reality for most nations by the end of the century, with the notable exception of many African countries, as shown here.. It seems clear that Canada’s recent population growth is largely driven by immigration, given the decline in natural population growth. Before diving deeper into immigration, it’s worth examining births and deaths as a percentage of the population at the provincial level to uncover potential regional differences within Canada.

Most provinces align with the national birth rate trend, except for Newfoundland & Labrador, which had a higher birth rate than the Canadian average until 1984, after which it has trended slightly lower. The territories have historically had much higher birth rates, with the Northwest Territories, including Nunavut, maintaining nearly double the birth rate of southern parts of the country until around 1990. However, birth rates across all regions are converging toward approximately 1 birth per hundred people per year, which is well below the replacement level. Nunavut still has the highest birth rate in Canada, but it declined from 2.45 to 1.74 births per hundred between 2020 and 2022. If this trend continues, Nunavut's birth rate may soon match that of the rest of the country within the next 2-3 years.

I was surprised to see that the Northwest Territories and Nunavut had such a high death rate in the 1950s and early 1960s, and equally surprised by the rapid decline that followed. In 1952, their death rate was more than double the national average, yet by 1964-65, it matched the national average and continued to drop below it in the 1970s. This high death rate can largely be attributed to the tuberculosis epidemic among the Inuit population from the 1940s to the 1960s (see more details here) and the lack of medical facilities in the north during that time. It would be interesting to investigate why the death rate in the Northwest Territories and Nunavut has remained lower than the national average since then. Possible factors could include improved access to medical care or more people from these areas moving away for retirement.

When examining immigration, a key driver of national population growth in the 21st century, we notice a cyclical pattern in immigration rates from the 1950s to the mid-1980s. After that, the pattern becomes more erratic, fluctuating between 0-3% of the overall population. In 2020, there was a sudden drop in immigration, likely due to COVID-19, followed by a dramatic increase over the next two years. British Columbia, for example, saw its immigration rate surge from 1.53 per hundred people in 2020 to 8.70 per hundred in 2022—a more than 500% increase in just two years. While it’s difficult to predict how long this trend will continue, it's clear that it’s having a significant impact on the country.

While internal migration doesn't significantly impact the overall population of the country, there is a substantial number of people moving within Canada. Some provinces experience net gains or losses of up to 100,000 individuals in a single year, while others see changes of less than 100 people. The provinces with the most interprovincial movement are Quebec, Ontario, Alberta, and British Columbia. It's fascinating how a province can quickly shift from being a popular destination to losing the most residents within just a few years. A deeper analysis could reveal not only the net gains and losses but also which provinces are exchanging residents.

While Quebec, Ontario, Alberta, and British Columbia have the highest numbers of interprovincial migrants, the movement between them is uneven. Most people leaving Quebec move to Ontario, while Ontarians primarily relocate to Quebec, British Columbia, and Alberta. Since 2020, there’s been a noticeable increase in people leaving Ontario for other parts of Canada, particularly New Brunswick and Nova Scotia, likely driven by rising housing costs in the Greater Toronto Area and the growth of remote work. Albertans tend to move to British Columbia, Ontario, and, to a lesser extent, Saskatchewan. Meanwhile, British Columbians are increasingly moving to Alberta, especially since 2020, possibly due to Vancouver’s escalating housing costs.

When analyzing this chart along with the previous one, it's clear that Ontario, Alberta, and British Columbia are the top destinations for internal migration. However, the gap between the number of people leaving and arriving in each province is smaller than expected. Additionally, Ontario and Alberta have much more migration exchange with the Atlantic provinces compared to Quebec or British Columbia, indicating that fewer people move between the east and west coasts.

This dashboard offers many insights, but here are some key observations. Generally, larger cities have a higher proportion of immigrants than smaller communities, though there are exceptions. Toronto leads with 46.16% of its population being immigrants, followed by Vancouver at 41.21%.

There are also notable differences in the age and origins of immigrants across major cities in Canada. Prairie cities tend to have more working-age immigrants, while Toronto and Vancouver have a more balanced age distribution. The origins of immigrants vary significantly by city as well. Quebec City and Montreal have a higher proportion of African immigrants, particularly from French-speaking countries. Coastal regions, especially Vancouver Island, see more immigrants from the UK and also have a larger retiree population.

In the Prairie provinces, Filipino immigrants are the largest group, with Winkler, MB, being an exception due to its significant Mexican and German immigrant populations. Sault Ste. Marie stands out for its large Italian population, which you can read about here

There are undoubtedly many more stories within this data that could be explored further, but for now, these are the most striking findings.
